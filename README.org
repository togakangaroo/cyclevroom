*** Motors, motor controllers, and power

**** Why you need a motor controller

Microcontroller GPIO pins output 3.3V at ~20mA — nowhere near enough to drive a DC motor (which wants hundreds of mA to amps). A motor controller sits between the MCU and motors and acts as a power amplifier. It also contains H-bridge circuitry that lets you reverse the motor direction by flipping the current, which is how you get both forward and backward motion on each wheel.

For independent wheel control and in-place turns, you need a *dual-channel* controller (or two single-channel ones) — one channel per wheel. In-place turns just mean: left motor full forward, right motor full reverse (or vice versa). That's the same differential drive logic, just taken to the extreme.

**** Recommended motor controllers at RC scale

| Controller  | Current (cont.) | Voltage  | Notes                                             |
|-------------+-----------------+----------+---------------------------------------------------|
| L298N       | 2A/ch           | 5-35V    | Classic, cheap (~$3), inefficient/runs hot        |
| TB6612FNG   | 1.2A/ch (3.2A peak) | 2.5-13.5V | Better than L298N, MOSFET-based, efficient, ~$5 |
| DRV8833     | 1.5A/ch         | 2.7-10.8V | Very small, common on breakout boards, ~$3      |
| Cytron MDD3A| 3A/ch           | 4.5-13.5V | Dual channel, good value if motors are beefier   |

For this project, the *TB6612FNG* is the right default — efficient, widely supported, fits the motor sizes we'd use. The L298N works but wastes power as heat, which matters for battery life.

**** Power topology

Two separate power rails is the standard pattern:

: [LiPo battery] ──→ [motor controller] ──→ [motors]
:                 └→ [5V/3.3V regulator] ──→ [MCU + sensors]

Never power the MCU directly from the motor supply — motor switching noise will reset or corrupt the MCU. Separate rails with a small buck converter or LDO regulator for the logic side.

*Battery:* A 2S LiPo (7.4V nominal) is the RC-car standard — light, high energy density, rechargeable. Pairs well with the TB6612FNG's voltage range. You'll want a LiPo-safe charger and should add a low-voltage cutoff to avoid over-discharging (kills LiPos).

**** PWM speed control

Motor speed is set by PWM signal from the MCU — the motor controller uses the duty cycle to throttle power to the motor. Typical setup per channel:

- One PWM pin → speed (0-100% duty cycle)
- One or two direction pins → forward/reverse

Most motor controller libraries handle this abstraction. On ESP32 or Pico, built-in hardware PWM makes this clean.

*** What is this called?

I still think it's important to figure out what this robot form factor is called so that we can look up guidance on how to best do it. The idea is that all the actual "stuff" (sensors, weapons, computer, drive train) is suspended between two larger wheels. So kinda like a tie fighter but with wheels instead of wings. This way, we don't have to deal with the possibility of getting turned upside down as much and it can turn in-place.

*It's called a diwheel (or dicycle).* Two large parallel wheels, body/frame riding between them — exactly the Tie Fighter analogy.

**** Key design insight: passive stability

If the body's center of mass sits *below* the wheel axle, the bot is passively stable like a pendulum — no IMU or balancing algorithm needed. This is the opposite of Segway-style "inverted pendulum" robots where the body sits above the axle and requires active control. At RC scale, passive stability is the right approach: put the batteries low and let gravity do the work.

**** The one problem to know about: gerbiling

During hard acceleration or braking, the inner frame can spin relative to the wheels (like a hamster wheel spinning without the hamster). At small scale this is much less of an issue — just keep CoM well below the axle and don't accelerate aggressively.

*** Napkin math: can a microcontroller actually detect the timing difference?

Speed of sound: ~343 m/s

Robot is ~1ft (30cm) wide, so mics are at most 30cm apart.

Max time difference (sound arrives perfectly from one side, 90°):
: Δt = 0.30m / 343 m/s ≈ 874 µs

For a given angle θ from the center line: =Δt = d × sin(θ) / v=

| Angle from center | Δt (30cm spacing) | Δt (15cm spacing) |
|-------------------+-------------------+-------------------|
| 90° (hard left)   | 874 µs            | 437 µs            |
| 45°               | 618 µs            | 309 µs            |
| 20°               | 299 µs            | 150 µs            |
| 10°               | 152 µs            | 76 µs             |
| 5°                | 76 µs             | 38 µs             |

*Can we detect this?* The timing differences are large enough to detect, but the platform choice matters a lot — see below.

**** Platform options for timing detection

*Arduino / ESP board (bare metal microcontroller)*
A bare metal microcontroller has no OS, so hardware interrupts fire with ~1-2 µs jitter. Arduino's =micros()= has 4 µs resolution; ESP32 is similar. The full 38-874 µs range is well within reach. ESP32 is the better pick over Arduino here: built-in WiFi means you can do OTA (over-the-air) code uploads and use a browser-based REPL — not quite SSH but closer than a raw Arduino. MicroPython runs on it too.

*Raspberry Pi (or similar Linux SBC)*
This is the appeal — SSH in, write Python, done. The problem: Linux is not a real-time OS. GPIO interrupt latency on a Pi is typically 50-200 µs, and it can spike unpredictably to 1000+ µs when the scheduler is doing other things. That jitter swamps the signal at finer angles (our 5° case is only 38-76 µs). For coarse direction (left vs right, >45°, Δt > 300 µs) it might work often enough to be useful. For reliable fine-grained direction, it won't.

One partial fix: the =pigpio= library on the Pi uses DMA-based GPIO sampling at configurable rates (down to 1 µs) rather than relying on the interrupt scheduler. It's much better than raw GPIO interrupts and might be good enough for this use case. Worth trying before giving up on the Pi entirely.

*Raspberry Pi Pico W*
A middle ground worth knowing about. The Pico (RP2040 chip) is a proper microcontroller — no Linux, real-time capable, interrupt jitter in the low microseconds. The Pico *W* adds WiFi. Supports MicroPython. Not SSH exactly, but you can use =mpremote= or Thonny over USB, and there's a WebREPL over WiFi. Much easier to develop on than a traditional Arduino while still being real-time.

*Recommended approach*
Start with an ESP32 or Pico W for the mic timing work — validate the concept. If the rest of the robot logic gets complex enough that you want a full Linux environment, add a Pi Zero 2W alongside it as the "brain" (handles SSH, higher-level logic, camera, etc.) and let the ESP32/Pico handle only the time-critical interrupt detection, communicating direction results over UART or I2C.

*Simpler approach than full audio sampling:* Don't try to record and cross-correlate waveforms. Instead, use each mic signal through a simple analog comparator (or op-amp) to trigger a digital interrupt when the sound exceeds a threshold. Record the timestamp of each interrupt. The time difference between the two timestamps is your direction signal. A clap is a sharp impulse — perfect for threshold detection.

*Three mics, TDOA triangulation:* With 3 mics in a triangle arrangement, you get 3 pairwise timing deltas (Δt₁₂, Δt₁₃, Δt₂₃ — only 2 are independent). Each delta defines a hyperbola of possible source positions. Two hyperbolas intersect to give a direction; the third overdetermines the system and can be used for error checking. This fully solves front/back ambiguity and gives better angular accuracy than two mics alone.

The mic positions relative to the robot center are the key inputs to the geometry. These need to be tunable parameters in the code — small errors in measured mic placement translate directly into directional error, and you'll want to calibrate against a known sound source rather than trust your ruler.

*Re: using speakers as mics* — technically possible (a speaker coil works in reverse), but sensitivity will be terrible. Use LM393 sound sensor modules (KY-038/FC-04) instead of raw capsules — they include the comparator circuit needed for interrupt-trigger detection and require no additional wiring or IC soldering.

*** Overall behavior loop

1. Hear clap → compute direction via TDOA across 3 mics
2. Turn in place toward the source (differential drive)
3. Drive forward; repeat direction updates from any subsequent claps to stay on target
4. Stop when close enough (TBD — fixed time, ultrasonic sensor, or just vibes)
5. Fire laser

*** Calibration parameters needed

- x/y position of each mic relative to robot center (measured, then fine-tuned)
- Per-mic timing offset (corrects for any systematic latency differences in the analog circuitry)
- Clap detection threshold (amplitude floor to ignore background noise)
- Blanking window duration (~5-10ms after first trigger to suppress echo re-triggers)

*** Acoustic environment

Real rooms produce echoes — a single clap generates a direct-path impulse plus reflections arriving within milliseconds. Comparator circuits will trigger multiple times per clap; a blanking window (~5-10ms) after each mic's first trigger suppresses the echo re-triggers.

The key insight that makes this tractable: *each clap only needs to give a coarse vote* — roughly which direction, and front-vs-back. The user can keep clapping, and the bot accumulates votes and corrects course. Individual noisy readings get averaged out over multiple claps. This is a fundamentally easier problem than single-shot precise localization.

At that accuracy requirement (coarse direction + front/back), 2 mics handle left/right and a 3rd breaks front/back ambiguity. Since electret capsules are $0.50 each, start with 3 and drop to 2 if the third isn't pulling its weight.

** Laser!

The laser fires forward, fixed mount — no servo needed, since the bot has already turned to face the target before firing.

*Simplest implementation:* A KY-008 laser diode module (~$1-2) is designed for microcontroller projects. Wire it through an NPN transistor or small MOSFET (2N2222 or 2N7000) — GPIO pin controls the transistor base, transistor switches the laser ground. Don't drive the laser directly from GPIO; it draws more current than a pin can safely source.

Hacking a handheld pointer also works (solder to the button contacts, switch with a transistor), just more fiddly than a dedicated module.

*Safety class:* Keep it to Class II (<1mW) or IIIa (<5mW). Since the bot autonomously points a laser at whatever it hears, don't go higher.

** Choosing no_std

Two flavors of embedded Rust for the ESP32:

- =no_std= (bare metal) via *esp-hal* -- no OS, direct hardware access. The mainstream, best-supported path for the C3 and what =esp-generate= scaffolds.
- =std= via *esp-idf-hal* -- runs on top of Espressif's ESP-IDF C framework, giving threads/heap/full networking more easily, at the cost of a much heavier toolchain.

Going with *no_std + esp-hal*. It's the natural fit for the interrupt-timing precision the mic TDOA work needs, and it's the better-documented path.

(Update: this held through the mic and motor steps, but once WiFi + OTA entered the picture the math changed -- see [[*Going wireless: switching to std Rust][Going wireless]] below.)

Important: =no_std= does *not* mean giving up WiFi/Bluetooth. =no_std= is about not having the standard library (heap/threads/filesystem), not about radios. On esp-hal, WiFi + BLE work in =no_std= via the *esp-wifi* crate, and pairing with *embassy* (an embedded async runtime) lets a radio task run alongside motor/mic tasks concurrently with no OS. So browser/phone control can be added later without switching off this base. =esp-generate= can include esp-wifi + embassy from the start, so we don't paint ourselves into a corner.

*** The approach

The near mic hears the clap first. So: race both D0 rising edges with embassy's =select=, timestamp whichever wins, then wait (bounded by a timeout) for the other edge and timestamp that too. The gap (=Δt=) tells us which side and by hpw much.

At 5cm spacing the *largest physically possible* arrival difference is only =0.05 / 343 ≈ 146 µs=, and that's for sound coming dead sideways. Two cheap KY-038 modules jitter by tens of µs on their own, so we don't pretend to resolve an angle -- we report just three states: *LEFT*, *RIGHT*, and *CENTER* (=Δt= below a deadband, too small to trust). Good enough: the real robot only needs a coarse "which way" vote per clap and corrects over repeated claps.

Code is in [[./firmware-left-or-right/src/bin/main.rs][main.rs]]. The tunables live at the top:
- =CENTER_DEADBAND_US= (60) -- below this |Δt|, call it CENTER. Clap from known left/right, watch the logged deltas, and set this just above the spread you see from center claps.
- =BLANKING_MS= (200) -- ignore echoes/chatter after each clap.
- =PAIR_TIMEOUT_MS= (50) -- how long to wait for the second mic before reporting on just the first.

*** Wiring

- LEFT mic D0 on *GPIO4* (where the single mic lived)
- RIGHT mic D0 goes to *GPIO5*.
- LED on *GPIO3*.


*** Deploy

*** L298N pinout and wiring

The L298N board exposes, per channel: two direction inputs and one enable input.

| L298N pin | Role                                    |
|-----------+-----------------------------------------|
| ENA       | Channel A enable / PWM speed (motor A)  |
| IN1, IN2  | Channel A direction (motor A)           |
| IN3, IN4  | Channel B direction (motor B)           |
| ENB       | Channel B enable / PWM speed (motor B)  |
| OUT1,OUT2 | Motor A leads                           |
| OUT3,OUT4 | Motor B leads                           |
| +12V      | Motor supply in (our ~7.4V from 18650s) |
| GND       | Common ground (battery *and* ESP32)     |
| +5V       | 5V *out* from onboard regulator         |

Direction truth table (per channel): =(IN1,IN2) = (H,L)= spins one way, =(L,H)=
the other, =(L,L)= brakes. "Forward" vs "reverse" depends on which way the motor
leads are soldered -- if a wheel runs backwards, swap that motor's two OUT leads.

*ENA/ENB jumpers:* pull the two shunt jumpers off the board so the ESP32 can
drive ENA/ENB with PWM for speed control (with them on, enables are tied HIGH =
full speed only).

*Power:* this test drives around untethered, so the *whole robot runs off the
battery* -- no USB. Battery + to =+12V=, battery - to =GND=. The L298N's onboard
regulator turns that into a clean 5V on its =+5V= pin, which we feed into the
ESP32-C3's =5V= pin to power the board. Ground is shared automatically through
the L298N =GND= (mandatory or the logic levels are meaningless).

The onboard regulator holds 5V as long as the 12V input is above ~7V, which our
~7.4V pack clears. Before connecting the C3, verify ~5V on the =+5V= pin with a
multimeter.

(The ENA/ENB jumpers are separate and unrelated to the regulator -- pull them, as
called out below, so the C3 can PWM the enables for speed control.)

⚠ *Do not plug in USB while the L298N 5V is feeding the board* -- two supplies
fighting on the same rail. Battery 5V and USB are mutually exclusive: unplug USB
before you connect the L298N =+5V=.

*ESP32-C3 → L298N pin map* (all clean GPIOs; the C3 strapping pins are 2/8/9,
none of which we use -- GPIO0 is a plain GPIO on the C3, unlike the classic ESP32
where it's a boot pin):

| Signal        | ESP32-C3 | L298N |
|---------------+----------+-------|
| Left speed    | GPIO0    | ENA   |
| Left dir 1    | GPIO1    | IN1   |
| Left dir 2    | GPIO10   | IN2   |
| Right dir 1   | GPIO7    | IN3   |
| Right dir 2   | GPIO5    | IN4   |
| Right speed   | GPIO6    | ENB   |
| Board power   | 5V       | +5V   |
| Ground        | GND      | GND   |

*** The code

Code is in [[./moving/src/bin/main.rs][main.rs]]. Speed comes from the ESP32's LEDC (hardware PWM)
peripheral driving ENA/ENB; direction from the four IN pins. A =Motor= struct
wraps one enable channel + its two direction pins with =forward/reverse/stop=,
and movement primitives (=straight=, =spin=) compose those. =drive_rectangle=
runs the leg/turn choreography.

Because there are no encoders, everything is dead-reckoned by *time* -- the
tunables at the top set leg durations and turn durations. Expect to twiddle:
- =DRIVE_DUTY= (40%) / =TURN_DUTY= (50%) -- if a wheel stalls, raise its duty;
  below ~30% the motors may not overcome stiction.
- =TURN_90_MS= (600) -- watch a spin and adjust until it lands near a right
  angle. The 180 about-face is just =2 * TURN_90_MS=.
- =LONG_LEG_MS= / =SHORT_LEG_MS= -- the straight distances.

The two motors won't spin at identical RPM (~20% mismatch is known and fine at
this stage -- the real robot corrects via mic votes, not dead-reckoning), so the
rectangle will drift. That's expected; this test is just proving wheel control,
speed, and the motion primitives.

*** Why std now

The [[*Choosing no_std][original no_std choice]] was right for the GPIO/timing steps, but WiFi + OTA
flips the trade. In no_std, WiFi means hand-wiring =esp-radio= + =embassy-net= +
=smoltcp= against sparsely documented APIs, and OTA means building your own
image-receive path and rollback logic. The =std= flavor (=esp-idf-hal= /
=esp-idf-svc=) runs on Espressif's ESP-IDF C framework underneath, which turns
all of that into supported product: =EspWifi= for the connection, plain
=std::net::TcpListener= for the server, =EspOta= wrapping ESP-IDF's tested
dual-slot OTA + rollback, and NVS flash storage for free (tuned constants can
persist across reboots).

The price is a much heavier toolchain: the first build downloads and compiles
ESP-IDF itself (a gig or so, tens of minutes). One-time
tax. The C3 being RISC-V spares us the forked-Xtensa-compiler pain -- plain
upstream nightly works.

Earlier steps stay no_std; each step is its own crate, so nothing breaks.
