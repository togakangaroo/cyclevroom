*** Motors, motor controllers, and power

**** Why you need a motor controller

Microcontroller GPIO pins output 3.3V at ~20mA — nowhere near enough to drive a DC motor (which wants hundreds of mA to amps). A motor controller sits between the MCU and motors and acts as a power amplifier. It also contains H-bridge circuitry that lets you reverse the motor direction by flipping the current, which is how you get both forward and backward motion on each wheel.

For independent wheel control and in-place turns, you need a *dual-channel* controller (or two single-channel ones) — one channel per wheel. In-place turns just mean: left motor full forward, right motor full reverse (or vice versa). That's the same differential drive logic, just taken to the extreme.

**** Recommended motor controllers at RC scale

| Controller  | Current (cont.) | Voltage  | Notes                                             |
|-------------+-----------------+----------+---------------------------------------------------|
| L298N       | 2A/ch           | 5-35V    | Classic, cheap (~$3), inefficient/runs hot        |
| TB6612FNG   | 1.2A/ch (3.2A peak) | 2.5-13.5V | Better than L298N, MOSFET-based, efficient, ~$5 |
| DRV8833     | 1.5A/ch         | 2.7-10.8V | Very small, common on breakout boards, ~$3      |
| Cytron MDD3A| 3A/ch           | 4.5-13.5V | Dual channel, good value if motors are beefier   |

For this project, the *TB6612FNG* is the right default — efficient, widely supported, fits the motor sizes we'd use. The L298N works but wastes power as heat, which matters for battery life.

**** Power topology

Two separate power rails is the standard pattern:

: [LiPo battery] ──→ [motor controller] ──→ [motors]
:                 └→ [5V/3.3V regulator] ──→ [MCU + sensors]

Never power the MCU directly from the motor supply — motor switching noise will reset or corrupt the MCU. Separate rails with a small buck converter or LDO regulator for the logic side.

*Battery:* A 2S LiPo (7.4V nominal) is the RC-car standard — light, high energy density, rechargeable. Pairs well with the TB6612FNG's voltage range. You'll want a LiPo-safe charger and should add a low-voltage cutoff to avoid over-discharging (kills LiPos).

**** PWM speed control

Motor speed is set by PWM signal from the MCU — the motor controller uses the duty cycle to throttle power to the motor. Typical setup per channel:

- One PWM pin → speed (0-100% duty cycle)
- One or two direction pins → forward/reverse

Most motor controller libraries handle this abstraction. On ESP32 or Pico, built-in hardware PWM makes this clean.

*** What is this called?

I still think it's important to figure out what this robot form factor is called so that we can look up guidance on how to best do it. The idea is that all the actual "stuff" (sensors, weapons, computer, drive train) is suspended between two larger wheels. So kinda like a tie fighter but with wheels instead of wings. This way, we don't have to deal with the possibility of getting turned upside down as much and it can turn in-place.

*It's called a diwheel (or dicycle).* Two large parallel wheels, body/frame riding between them — exactly the Tie Fighter analogy.

**** Key design insight: passive stability

If the body's center of mass sits *below* the wheel axle, the bot is passively stable like a pendulum — no IMU or balancing algorithm needed. This is the opposite of Segway-style "inverted pendulum" robots where the body sits above the axle and requires active control. At RC scale, passive stability is the right approach: put the batteries low and let gravity do the work.

**** The one problem to know about: gerbiling

During hard acceleration or braking, the inner frame can spin relative to the wheels (like a hamster wheel spinning without the hamster). At small scale this is much less of an issue — just keep CoM well below the axle and don't accelerate aggressively.

**** Notable examples and references

- [[https://newatlas.com/edward-a-diwheel-student-built-vehicle-that-really-works/18853/][EDWARD]] — Electric Diwheel With Active Rotation Damping, University of Adelaide student build (2011). Full-sized human-rideable, best documented diwheel engineering writeup available.
- [[https://hackaday.com/2025/10/07/building-a-diwheel-to-add-more-tank-controls-to-your-commute/][Sam Barker's DIY diwheel (Hackaday 2025)]] — Steel-welded frame, 3D-printed nylon rollers between inner frame and wheels, suspension added.
- [[https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicycle][Wikipedia: Dicycle]] — Overview, history, and variant list.

*** Napkin math: can a microcontroller actually detect the timing difference?

Speed of sound: ~343 m/s

Robot is ~1ft (30cm) wide, so mics are at most 30cm apart.

Max time difference (sound arrives perfectly from one side, 90°):
: Δt = 0.30m / 343 m/s ≈ 874 µs

For a given angle θ from the center line: =Δt = d × sin(θ) / v=

| Angle from center | Δt (30cm spacing) | Δt (15cm spacing) |
|-------------------+-------------------+-------------------|
| 90° (hard left)   | 874 µs            | 437 µs            |
| 45°               | 618 µs            | 309 µs            |
| 20°               | 299 µs            | 150 µs            |
| 10°               | 152 µs            | 76 µs             |
| 5°                | 76 µs             | 38 µs             |

*Can we detect this?* The timing differences are large enough to detect, but the platform choice matters a lot — see below.

**** Platform options for timing detection

*Arduino / ESP board (bare metal microcontroller)*
A bare metal microcontroller has no OS, so hardware interrupts fire with ~1-2 µs jitter. Arduino's =micros()= has 4 µs resolution; ESP32 is similar. The full 38-874 µs range is well within reach. ESP32 is the better pick over Arduino here: built-in WiFi means you can do OTA (over-the-air) code uploads and use a browser-based REPL — not quite SSH but closer than a raw Arduino. MicroPython runs on it too.

*Raspberry Pi (or similar Linux SBC)*
This is the appeal — SSH in, write Python, done. The problem: Linux is not a real-time OS. GPIO interrupt latency on a Pi is typically 50-200 µs, and it can spike unpredictably to 1000+ µs when the scheduler is doing other things. That jitter swamps the signal at finer angles (our 5° case is only 38-76 µs). For coarse direction (left vs right, >45°, Δt > 300 µs) it might work often enough to be useful. For reliable fine-grained direction, it won't.

One partial fix: the =pigpio= library on the Pi uses DMA-based GPIO sampling at configurable rates (down to 1 µs) rather than relying on the interrupt scheduler. It's much better than raw GPIO interrupts and might be good enough for this use case. Worth trying before giving up on the Pi entirely.

*Raspberry Pi Pico W*
A middle ground worth knowing about. The Pico (RP2040 chip) is a proper microcontroller — no Linux, real-time capable, interrupt jitter in the low microseconds. The Pico *W* adds WiFi. Supports MicroPython. Not SSH exactly, but you can use =mpremote= or Thonny over USB, and there's a WebREPL over WiFi. Much easier to develop on than a traditional Arduino while still being real-time.

*Recommended approach*
Start with an ESP32 or Pico W for the mic timing work — validate the concept. If the rest of the robot logic gets complex enough that you want a full Linux environment, add a Pi Zero 2W alongside it as the "brain" (handles SSH, higher-level logic, camera, etc.) and let the ESP32/Pico handle only the time-critical interrupt detection, communicating direction results over UART or I2C.

*Simpler approach than full audio sampling:* Don't try to record and cross-correlate waveforms. Instead, use each mic signal through a simple analog comparator (or op-amp) to trigger a digital interrupt when the sound exceeds a threshold. Record the timestamp of each interrupt. The time difference between the two timestamps is your direction signal. A clap is a sharp impulse — perfect for threshold detection.

*The front/back problem:* Two mics on opposite ends can tell you left vs right (which mic fired first) and roughly how far off-center (how big the time difference is), but a sound directly in front and directly behind both give Δt ≈ 0. You'd need a third mic (triangle arrangement) to fully disambiguate. For a first version, could just ignore it — the bot picks left or right and the person walks around to be in front if needed.

*Re: using speakers as mics* — technically possible (a speaker coil works in reverse), but sensitivity will be terrible. A cheap electret capsule ($0.50 each) is the right tool.
